Are We in a Tech Bubble? [Infographic]

Jul. 26 2011 - 9:03 am | 3,078 views | 0 recommendations | 7 comments
By JON BRUNER

A Google Trends graph showing frequency of searches for "Tech Bubble"

Observers have been asking for a few years now whether we’re getting into another Tech bubble like the valuation run-up of the late 1990s, but the question never struck me too viscerally until Google offered $6 billion to acquire Groupon last fall–and Groupon declined. Since then, talk of a tech bubble has accelerated: the Google Trends graph above shows that searches for “tech bubble” were very infrequent until the Groupon offer in November 2010, and they’ve been sustained since then, spiking at LinkedIn’s May 2011 IPO.

(Media mentions have followed a different pattern, according to my own analysis of Google News search results: they peaked in October 2008 as columnists compared the contemporary crisis to the bursting of the late-nineties tech bubble. My graph, which I created using a homemade script that counts Google News search results month by month for a particular phrase, doesn’t account for changes in Google News coverage over the search period, so I’m going to leave it in the background.)

KISSMetrics, a social media analytics firm whose founder, Hiten Shah, I interviewed for a story about Facebook, and FeeFighters, which helps businesses find credit-card processing contracts, have put together a compelling infographic that suggests tech stocks are not in a bubble–at least not one as large as the bubble of 1999-2000. By several measures, less is being invested in tech companies by venture capitalists and the stock market than around the turn of the century. At the same time, the Internet has a vastly larger user base, so higher valuations might be warranted. It’s an interesting and well-reasoned argument. But, of course, bubbles can be smaller than the late-nineties tech bubble and still burst on short notice.

[via KISSMetrics and FeeFighters]
